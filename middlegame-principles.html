Middle Game Principles

The middlegame is where chess becomes truly complex and creative. After completing development, you must formulate plans, coordinate your pieces, and create threats. These principles will help you navigate the middlegame with confidence and purpose!
1
Always Have a Plan
Never move without a purpose. After the opening, ask yourself: "What is my plan?" Common plans include: attacking the king, improving piece positions, creating pawn breaks, exploiting weaknesses, or controlling key squares. If you don't have a plan, improve your worst-placed piece.
Sample Plans:
Attack the kingside with pawns and pieces
Create a passed pawn on the queenside
Improve knight from b1 to d5 via c3 and e4
Random moves without strategic purpose
STRATEGY
2
Attack Weaknesses
Look for weak pawns and squares in your opponent's position. Weaknesses can't be defended by pawns: isolated pawns, backward pawns, doubled pawns, and squares that can't be controlled by pawns (holes). Direct your pieces toward these targets.
Common Weaknesses:
Target isolated d-pawn with pieces
Occupy hole on d5 with a knight
Attack doubled pawns on the c-file
Ignoring opponent's weaknesses and creating your own
STRATEGY
3
Create Outposts for Knights
An outpost is a square deep in enemy territory that can't be attacked by pawns. Knights are especially powerful on outposts (usually on the 5th or 6th rank) where they control key squares and can't be easily driven away. Support your knight on an outpost with pawns.
Example:
Knight on d5 supported by e4 pawn (classic outpost)
Knight on f5 attacking e7, d6, h6 (kingside outpost)
Knight on e5 that can be kicked with ...f6
PIECE PLACEMENT
4
Good Knight vs Bad Bishop
A "bad bishop" is blocked by its own pawns on the same color squares. A knight can be superior to such a bishop. When you have a bad bishop, try to exchange it or place your pawns on the opposite color. When facing a bad bishop, fix their pawns on that color.
Example:
Bishop on c8 with pawns on d5, e6, f7 (bad - place on c1 first)
Fix opponent's pawns on light squares when they have light-squared bishop
Keeping a bad bishop when you could trade it
PIECE EVALUATION
5
Two Bishops Are Powerful
Having both bishops (the "bishop pair") is an advantage, especially in open positions. The two bishops control all colors and work well together. Try to keep your bishop pair and trade your opponent's bishops for your knights when possible.
Strategy:
Keep both bishops in open positions with few pawns
Trade knight for bishop when you keep the bishop pair
Trading away bishops without compensation in open positions
PIECE EVALUATION
6
Coordinate Your Pieces
Pieces should work together toward a common goal. Uncoordinated pieces are easy to attack. Before launching an attack, make sure your pieces are supporting each other. This is called piece harmony. Doubled rooks, bishops aiming at the king, and pieces supporting advanced pawns are examples.
Example:
Rooks doubled on the d-file create immense pressure
Queen and bishop battery aimed at h7
Pieces scattered on unrelated squares with no coordination
PIECE COORDINATION
7
Space Advantage = More Options
The player who controls more squares has a space advantage. With more space, your pieces have more mobility and options. To gain space, advance pawns safely and control the center. When you have less space, trade pieces to reduce cramping.
Example:
Pawns on e5, d4, f4 give you space advantage
Cramped position? Trade pieces to create room
Advancing pawns recklessly and creating weaknesses
POSITIONAL PLAY
8
Attack on the Side Where You're Stronger
If you have more pieces on the kingside, attack there. If you have a pawn majority on the queenside, push those pawns. Don't attack where your opponent is stronger—you'll waste time and resources. Direct your forces where you have an advantage.
Example:
4 pieces near enemy king → Attack kingside
Queenside pawn majority → Push b4-b5-b6
Attacking on kingside with only your queen while opponent has 4 defenders
ATTACK
9
Open Lines Toward the Enemy King
To successfully attack the king, you need open lines for your heavy pieces (rooks and queen). Use pawn breaks to open files and diagonals. Common breaks include f4-f5, g4-g5 on the kingside, or b4-b5, c4-c5 on the queenside.
Example:
f4-f5 to open the f-file for your rook
h4-h5-hxg6 to expose enemy king
Trying to attack without opening any lines
ATTACK
10
Don't Attack Without Enough Pieces
A successful king attack usually requires at least 3-4 attacking pieces. Attacking with only one or two pieces rarely works. Bring up reinforcements before launching your assault. If you can't get enough pieces to the attack, look for a different plan.
Example:
Queen, both rooks, and bishop attacking → Strong attack
Only queen and one rook attacking → Easy to defend
Not enough attackers? Play on the other side of the board
ATTACK
11
When Attacked, Trade Pieces and Create Counterplay
If your opponent attacks your king, don't panic. Trade off their attacking pieces to reduce the pressure. Also, look for counterplay on the opposite side—attack their king or win material elsewhere. This forces them to slow down their attack to defend.
Defensive Strategy:
Trade your knight for their attacking bishop
Under attack on kingside? Counter-attack on queenside
Passively defending without creating any threats
DEFENSE
12
Improve Your Worst Piece
When you don't have an obvious plan, look at your pieces and identify the worst-placed one. Improve its position. Is a knight on the rim? Reroute it to the center. Is a bishop blocked? Reposition it. Is a rook passive? Find an open file. This simple principle always gives you something useful to do.
Example:
Knight on a3? Reroute via b1-d2-f3-e5
Rook on a1 with no open files? Swing to kingside via a3-g3
Leaving poorly placed pieces inactive throughout the game
PIECE IMPROVEMENT
13
Prophylaxis: Stop Opponent's Plans
Don't just focus on your own plans—pay attention to what your opponent wants to do! If you see your opponent is planning something dangerous, prevent it. This is called prophylaxis (preventive thinking). Ask: "What is my opponent threatening? How can I stop it?"
Example:
Opponent wants to play ...e5? Play d5 to prevent it
Opponent's knight heading to d5? Control that square with pawns
Only thinking about your plans and being surprised by opponent's ideas
PREVENTIVE THINKING
14
Control Key Squares
In the middlegame, certain squares become critically important based on the pawn structure—usually squares in or near the center that can't be attacked by pawns. Fight to control these squares with pieces, and occupy them when possible. Controlling key squares restricts your opponent's pieces.
Example:
Control d5 square to prevent opponent's pieces from occupying it
Dominate the e4 square in a French Defense structure
Allowing opponent's pieces to freely occupy strong central squares
POSITIONAL PLAY
15
Calculate Forcing Sequences
Before making a tactical move, calculate forcing sequences (checks, captures, threats) to see if your idea works. Calculate your opponent's best defenses. Don't rely on hope—verify with concrete calculation. This prevents blunders and helps you find winning combinations.
Calculation Method:
1. Identify candidate moves (checks, captures, threats)
2. Calculate forcing variations 3-5 moves deep
3. Look for opponent's best defense
Making tactical moves based on instinct without calculating
CALCULATION
